\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Optimizer interfaces as semantic interfaces.}  Public optimizer claims should name action, guard, layer, placement, decision time, and evidence surface.  These fields keep adapters, remote sources, and service stages from collapsing into one phenomenon.  The point is not to make every comparison more complicated; it is to make the denominator explicit enough that a reader can tell whether a reported difference is about the same public behavior.

\textbf{What changes in an evaluation.}  Before ranking latency, cardinality error, or plan quality, a study can test whether compared systems share the same public contract relation.  If not, it should retain separating fields, narrow the claim, or report mixed phenomena.  Tables~\ref{tab:anti-overfit-audit} and~\ref{tab:resource-profile} are meant to make this check cheap enough to rerun: the repair evidence is frozen before counting, and the replay cost is small compared with a full system-performance campaign.

\textbf{Protocol, scope, and refresh.}  JOB, TPC-H, and TPC-DS standardize inputs and metrics, not contract equality.  Every optimizer comparison is still a projection: choose a vocabulary, extract public contracts, validate the denominator, compute the kernel, and publish retained fields.  Counts are lower bounds over public evidence; later evidence becomes a source refresh.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

System R separates access paths and costing~\cite{selinger1979access}; Volcano and Cascades expose rule search as an optimizer object~\cite{graefe1990volcano,graefe1995cascades}; Calcite and Orca show why adapters and extensible rules belong in the optimizer interface~\cite{begoli2018calcite,soliman2014orca}.  Garlic and BigDAWG make placement a query-processing concern~\cite{carey1995garlic,stonebraker2015bigdawg}, while Snowflake and Dremel make cloud stages and serving trees visible at the public plan surface~\cite{dageville2016snowflake,melnik2010dremel}.  Workload benchmarks such as TPC-H~\cite{tpch}, TPC-DS~\cite{tpcds}, JOB~\cite{leis2015good}, and CardBench~\cite{chronis2024cardbench} standardize inputs or estimates.  SQL-equivalence and testing tools, including Cosette~\cite{chu2017cosette}, QED~\cite{wang2024qed}, SQLancer~\cite{rigger2020sqlancer}, and NoREC~\cite{rigger2020norec}, check semantics or wrong-answer behavior.  These lines of work supply the objects and controls that OptSem-C uses, but they do not define when two public optimizer-contract labels are equal.

\section{Conclusion}
OptSem-C shows that denominator validity must precede cross-system optimizer ranking.  A public label such as \texttt{pushdown}, \texttt{join}, or \texttt{cost based} is useful shorthand only after the comparison has preserved the fields that make the label comparable.  By fixing the schema, corpus, projection portfolio, repair universe, and replay checks before witness counting, OptSem-C turns that requirement into a finite audit rather than an informal caveat.  The resulting message is practical for VLDB-style evaluation: first certify the public contract denominator, then interpret latency, cardinality, or plan-quality differences on top of a comparison that names what it actually compares.
