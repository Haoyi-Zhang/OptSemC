\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Scope and protocol.}  Public optimizer claims should name action, guard, layer, placement, decision time, and evidence surface; otherwise adapters, remote sources, and service stages collapse into one phenomenon.  Before ranking latency, cardinality error, or plan quality, a study should test whether compared systems share the same public contract relation, then retain separating fields, narrow the claim, or report mixed phenomena.  JOB, TPC-H, and TPC-DS standardize inputs and metrics, not contract equality; OptSem-C's counts remain lower bounds over public evidence.

\textbf{Boundary of the claim.}  OptSem-C does not infer private cost models, prove workload-performance dominance, or turn a finite public corpus into a population guarantee.  Its object is the public contract denominator.  If two systems collapse under a comparison vocabulary while their source-supported contracts differ, the downstream latency, cardinality, or plan-quality comparison must either retain the separating fields or narrow the statement.  This boundary is why the repair and resource tables report failed point-learned engine-pair stress, source sensitivity, and deterministic replay cost instead of counting every stress row as a success.  The evidence is strongest where the denominator is fixed before counting: keyword and operator projections collide, layer and placement repair them in the admitted corpus, and the finite replay remains cheap enough to audit.  Broader corpora or private optimizer behavior require a new admission step, not extrapolation from these rows.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

System R separates access paths and costing~\cite{selinger1979access}; Volcano and Cascades expose rule search~\cite{graefe1990volcano,graefe1995cascades}; Calcite and Orca make adapters and extensible rules visible~\cite{begoli2018calcite,soliman2014orca}; and Garlic, BigDAWG, Snowflake, and Dremel surface placement and service stages~\cite{carey1995garlic,stonebraker2015bigdawg,dageville2016snowflake,melnik2010dremel}.  TPC-H~\cite{tpch}, TPC-DS~\cite{tpcds}, JOB~\cite{leis2015good}, and CardBench~\cite{chronis2024cardbench} standardize inputs or estimates, while Cosette~\cite{chu2017cosette}, QED~\cite{wang2024qed}, SQLancer~\cite{rigger2020sqlancer}, and NoREC~\cite{rigger2020norec} check equivalence or wrong answers.  These lines supply objects and controls, but not equality for public optimizer-contract labels.

\section{Conclusion}
OptSem-C shows that denominator validity must precede cross-system optimizer ranking.  Public labels such as \texttt{pushdown}, \texttt{join}, and \texttt{cost based} are useful only after the comparison preserves the fields that make them comparable.  Fixing the schema, corpus, projections, repairs, and replay checks before witness counting turns that requirement into a finite audit: certify the public contract denominator first, then interpret latency, cardinality, or plan-quality differences.
