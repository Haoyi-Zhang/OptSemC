\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Scope and protocol.}  Public optimizer claims should name action, guard, layer, placement, decision time, and evidence surface; otherwise adapters, remote sources, and service stages collapse into one phenomenon.  Before ranking latency, cardinality error, or plan quality, a study should test that shared contract relation, then retain separating fields, narrow the claim, or report mixed phenomena.  JOB, TPC-H, and TPC-DS standardize inputs and metrics, not contract equality.  OptSem-C is therefore a pre-performance denominator audit: it says when two public optimizer claims are safe to compare, not which engine will run a workload faster.

\textbf{Boundary of the claim.}  OptSem-C does not infer private cost models, rank workload performance, or turn a finite public corpus into a population guarantee.  Failed engine-pair stress, source sensitivity, and replay cost stay visible; broader corpora, proprietary workloads, future engine versions, and operator families outside the declared schema require a new admission step.  The resource tables measure replayable comparison stages and SQL-denominator checks; they are not a claim about full artifact setup or arbitrary benchmark execution.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Optimizer architectures supply the schema fields: System R, Volcano/Cascades, Calcite/Orca, Garlic, BigDAWG, Snowflake, and Dremel expose access paths, rule search, adapters, placement, and service stages~\cite{selinger1979access,graefe1990volcano,graefe1995cascades,begoli2018calcite,soliman2014orca,carey1995garlic,stonebraker2015bigdawg,dageville2016snowflake,melnik2010dremel}.  OptSem-C uses those dimensions as public-contract fields, not as a claim to observe private optimizer state.

Workload and optimizer benchmarks ask different questions.  TPC-H and TPC-DS define analytical workloads~\cite{tpch,tpcds}; JOB exposes join-order and cardinality-estimation pressure~\cite{leis2015good,leis2018looking}; CardEst and CardBench evaluate cardinality-estimation effects~\cite{han2021cardest,chronis2024cardbench}; Neo and Bao study learned plan choice or learned hinting~\cite{marcus2019neo,marcus2021bao}.  OptSem-C is complementary: it checks whether the public labels used to compare such systems preserve the same contract semantics before those workloads or plan-quality metrics are interpreted.

SQL equivalence and testing systems such as Cosette, QED, SQLancer, and NoREC target query semantics or DBMS bugs~\cite{chu2017cosette,wang2024qed,rigger2020sqlancer,rigger2020norec}.  They can show that two SQL expressions are equivalent or that a DBMS returns a suspicious result, but they do not define equality for public optimizer-contract labels such as pushdown, runtime adaptation, or plan evidence.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusion.} OptSem-C checks the public-contract denominator that must hold before optimizer labels can support latency, cardinality, or plan-quality comparisons.
