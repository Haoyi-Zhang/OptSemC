\section{Discussion, Limitations, and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Use, threats, and conclusion.}  OptSem-C audits finite public contracts, not optimizer correctness: zero witnesses do not imply private cost, prevalence, or population generalization.  Its single-extractor ledger is inspectable evidence, not inter-rater reliability; the replay package exports a recoding worksheet for an independent-coding follow-up, and the paper does not measure how often published comparisons use these projections.  It complements System R access paths~\cite{selinger1979access}, JOB workload tests~\cite{leis2018looking}, and SQLancer bug finding~\cite{rigger2020sqlancer}.  Stronger claims keep layer, placement, and evidence surface or state the coarser scope.

The audit should therefore be read as a denominator certificate, not a survey of optimizer practice.  A missing witness can mean that the public record lacks enough evidence for the declared contract; a present witness means that the chosen comparison vocabulary has dropped a field that the claim still needs.  The strongest empirical lesson is not that every optimizer comparison is unsafe, but that federated, cloud-service, connector, and runtime-adaptive boundaries need more precise labels than local enablement switches.

In practice, use OptSem-C before latency or portability claims: name the projection, inspect collapsed signatures, and keep separating fields.  Independent recoding and a retrospective audit of published comparison tables are the next prevalence tests; this paper supplies the replayable denominator machinery.
