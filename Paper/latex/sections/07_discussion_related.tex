\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Use and boundaries.}  OptSem-C is a pre-ranking audit.  Before comparing pushdown, join processing, materialization, adaptivity, or plan evidence, a study should name the action, guard, optimizer layer, execution placement, decision time, and evidence surface being compared.  A zero row is not an optimizer-correctness theorem; it only says that the tested public vocabulary did not collapse unequal reference signatures in this finite relation.  The work does not infer private cost models, rank runtime performance, or turn public documentation into a population guarantee.  New engines, proprietary traces, undocumented switches, and out-of-schema operators require new admission and replay; source removal is information deletion, so retained and induced witnesses are reported separately.

\section{Related Work and Conclusion}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Optimizers and analytical systems.}  System R separates access paths and costing~\cite{selinger1979access}.  Volcano supplies rule search~\cite{graefe1990volcano}; its generator makes search programmable~\cite{graefe1993volcano}; and Cascades gives the memo model~\cite{graefe1995cascades}.  Calcite~\cite{begoli2018calcite} and Orca~\cite{soliman2014orca} expose framework boundaries.  C-Store~\cite{stonebraker2005cstore}, column-store comparisons~\cite{abadi2008columnstores}, compression~\cite{abadi2006compression}, materialization~\cite{abadi2007materialization}, and a column-store survey~\cite{abadi2013columnoriented} show why representation matters.  Vectorwise~\cite{zukowski2012vectorwise}, HyPer~\cite{kemper2011hyper}, Hive~\cite{thusoo2009hive}, Impala~\cite{kornacker2015impala}, Garlic~\cite{carey1995garlic}, BigDAWG~\cite{stonebraker2015bigdawg}, Dremel~\cite{melnik2010dremel}, Snowflake~\cite{dageville2016snowflake}, Redshift~\cite{gupta2015redshift}, and F1~\cite{shute2013f1} expose placement or service boundaries.

\textbf{Benchmarks.}  TPC decision-support benchmarking~\cite{poess2000tpc}, TPC-DS~\cite{nambiar2006tpcds}, SSB~\cite{oneil2009ssb}, Wisconsin~\cite{bitton1983wisconsin}, and data-warehouse benchmark surveys~\cite{darmont2007benchmarking} standardize workload ideas.  JOB~\cite{leis2015good} targets join-order behavior.  Pavlo et al. compare large-scale analysis systems~\cite{pavlo2009comparison}; YCSB studies cloud serving~\cite{cooper2010ycsb}; and LakeBench~\cite{deng2024lakebench} stresses lake discovery.  CardEst~\cite{han2021cardest}, CardBench~\cite{chronis2024cardbench}, Kipf et al.'s learned estimator~\cite{kipf2019learned}, NeuroCard~\cite{yang2019neurocard}, DeepDB~\cite{hilprecht2020deepdb}, and FactorJoin~\cite{wu2023factorjoin} evaluate cardinality or distribution models.  Neo~\cite{marcus2019neo} and Bao~\cite{marcus2021bao} test learned planning.  OptSem-C asks whether public optimizer behaviors grouped under a label are the same public contract.

\textbf{Testing and evidence.}  SQLancer~\cite{rigger2020sqlancer} and NoREC~\cite{rigger2020norec} search for DBMS and optimization bugs.  Cosette~\cite{chu2017cosette}, semiring axioms~\cite{chu2018usemiring}, QED~\cite{wang2024qed}, containment~\cite{chandra1977optimal}, and relational equivalence~\cite{aho1979equivalences} supply SQL verification background.  Why/where provenance~\cite{buneman2001why}, semiring provenance~\cite{green2007provenance}, provenance surveys~\cite{cheney2009provenance}, and annotated containment~\cite{green2011containment} keep evidence in reasoning.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusion.} OptSem-C makes public optimizer labels auditable before runtime rankings.
