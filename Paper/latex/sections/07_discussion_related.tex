\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Optimizer interfaces as semantic interfaces.}  Public optimizer claims should name action, guard, layer, placement, decision time, and evidence surface.  These fields do not expose private cost formulas; they keep adapters, remote sources, and service stages from collapsing into one phenomenon, a boundary visible in federation~\cite{carey1995garlic} and polystores~\cite{stonebraker2015bigdawg}.

\textbf{What changes in an evaluation.}  Before ranking latency, cardinality error, or plan quality, a study can test whether compared systems share the same public contract relation.  If a table equates connector delegation with runtime filtering, the study should retain separating fields, narrow the claim, or report mixed phenomena.

\textbf{Protocol, scope, and refresh.}  JOB, TPC-H, and TPC-DS standardize inputs and metrics, not contract equality.  Every optimizer comparison is still a projection: choose a vocabulary, extract public contracts, validate the denominator, compute the kernel, and publish retained fields.  Counts are lower bounds over public evidence; later evidence becomes a source refresh.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

OptSem-C sits between optimizer architecture, benchmarks, and DBMS testing.  System R introduced access paths and costed search~\cite{selinger1979access}; Volcano~\cite{graefe1990volcano} and Cascades~\cite{graefe1995cascades} exposed extensible rule search; Calcite~\cite{begoli2018calcite} and Orca~\cite{soliman2014orca} make adapter and modular optimizer boundaries explicit.  Federation and polystore systems show why placement matters~\cite{carey1995garlic,stonebraker2015bigdawg}; cloud engines expose further service and plan-evidence boundaries~\cite{dageville2016snowflake,melnik2010dremel}.  These lines explain the schema dimensions, but they do not define when two public optimizer claims are safe to compare as equal.

Benchmarks and testing answer neighboring questions.  TPC-H~\cite{tpch}, TPC-DS~\cite{tpcds}, JOB~\cite{leis2015good}, and CardBench~\cite{chronis2024cardbench} standardize workloads or estimation stress.  Cosette~\cite{chu2017cosette} and QED~\cite{wang2024qed} check SQL equivalence; SQLancer~\cite{rigger2020sqlancer} and NoREC~\cite{rigger2020norec} expose DBMS bugs.  OptSem-C instead audits the public contract denominator that such studies implicitly rely on before ranking latency, plan quality, or correctness evidence across systems.

\section{Conclusion}
OptSem-C shows that coarse optimizer labels can equate different public contracts, so denominator validity must precede performance ranking.
