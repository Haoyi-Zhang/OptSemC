\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}
We evaluate four checks: SQL-denominator validity, projection collisions, field repairs under stress, and replay cost.  Inputs are frozen before counting: schema, projections, source manifest, admission codebook, and retained fields.  Exhaustive field search is a certificate check; layer and placement come from architecture.  Execution validates probes only: it never admits rules, chooses fields, or feeds collision tables.  ``Coll.'' is contract-level: the vocabulary collapses unequal public optimizer-contract signatures before any latency, plan-quality, or cost claim is interpreted.

The comparison surfaces are executable projection vocabularies and controls, not DBMS bug finders such as SQLancer~\cite{rigger2020sqlancer}, NoREC~\cite{rigger2020norec}, or other SQL fuzzing tools.  Those tools test execution behavior under generated oracles; OptSem-C audits whether a public comparison vocabulary has already collapsed unequal contracts.  The projection portfolio is practice-facing but finite: across the 26 public source surfaces an engine user or comparison paper can read, all expose keyword-style or operator-family labels, twelve expose yes/no-style controls, and none exposes the complete reference contract payload.  Reference comparison is the negative control, one-field projections are diagnostic ablations, and strengthened projections restore missing fields.  All projections are fixed before witness counting and evaluated over the same source-linked maps; \texttt{optsemc witnesses} prints the rows behind Table~\ref{tab:contract-collision}, and \texttt{optsemc audit-csv} applies the same test to an external contract-atom table.  The question is how many witnesses are lost if equality is decided at that public vocabulary, not whether every cited study made that exact projection.

\textbf{Coarse comparison induces concentrated, separable collisions.} The main empirical result is a finite counterexample to two coarse optimizer-comparison vocabularies, plus a positive result about narrower controls.  Table~\ref{tab:contract-collision} keeps public vocabularies, controls, stress rows, and separating fields on the same scale: keyword declares 2,284 equivalences and collapses 254 unequal reference signatures, while operator-only declares 2,268 and collapses 238.  These 11.1\% and 10.5\% fractions condition on equivalences declared by the tested vocabularies inside this corpus.  The 492 primary records are concentrated: keyword records are BigQuery--ClickHouse (203), BigQuery--Trino (45), and ClickHouse--Trino (6); operator-only records are ClickHouse--Trino (123) and Spark SQL--Trino (115).  The remaining 17 engine pairs have zero headline collisions, so coarse labels often suffice for local-engine or non-boundary comparisons in this denominator.  The risk signal appears at cloud service, connector, distributed execution, and runtime-adaptation boundaries where public layer and placement distinctions carry the comparison.  The yes/no public-control view declares 2,036 equivalences but collapses only six, all in ClickHouse--Trino, a narrow positive result for enablement-style controls.  The strict reference and strengthened surface controls have zero collapsed pairs; one-field stress rows show that a single optimizer dimension can make the denominator worse rather than safer.

The collisions are not merely alternative names for the same behavior.  Table~\ref{tab:claim-severity} reclassifies the collapsed rows by the downstream claim type that would need the missing field.  Every headline witness is disjoint at the reference-signature level.  All 254 keyword witnesses differ in placement, and 248 also differ in decision time and observability; a pushdown or explain comparison would therefore be unsafe for portability, runtime-behavior, or plan-evidence claims unless those fields are retained.  All 238 operator-only witnesses differ in optimizer layer, 206 differ in placement, and 160 differ in state, so an operator column is not specific enough for architecture ranking or enablement explanations at the observed boundary pairs.  The six yes/no witnesses are different: they are all placement/layer/state boundary cases and do not involve time or observability, which is why the paper treats binary controls as mostly safe in this catalog rather than as a broad danger signal.

The measured public-surface catalog makes these projections realistic comparison targets but not prevalence estimates.  All 26 admitted sources expose keyword or operator-family labels, twelve expose yes/no controls, and none exposes full reference signatures.  Source-rich engines create more measured opportunities, so pair counts must be read through source sensitivity: PostgreSQL and Trino contribute 27.9\% and 21.3\% of rules, while DuckDB and Snowflake contribute 7.3\% and 9.1\%.  The source-density ledger reports raw and normalized hotspots: keyword peaks at BigQuery--ClickHouse with 203 witnesses, 3.22 per rule-mass unit, and 0.211 per rule-product unit; operator-only peaks at ClickHouse--Trino with 123, 1.41, and 0.078.  If a comparison falls outside this finite catalog, rerun extraction rather than transfer the certificate.

Finite resampling keeps keyword at roughly 10--12\% and operator labels at roughly 9--12\% of corpus-specific declared equivalences.  Single-source removal is stricter to read: removing one source can delete original witnesses and create new ones, so the audit reports both nonzero counts and witness-identity movement rather than treating leave-one-source as population sampling.  The diagnosis column localizes the missing semantics: placement separates keyword, layer separates the operator row, and the strengthened semantic surface returns to zero collapsed pairs.

\textbf{Projection kernels are measurable objects.} Figure~\ref{fig:projection-information} reports the finite information profile.  Reference signatures form 1,288 observed classes.  Keyword projection compresses them into 148 projected classes, retains 64\% of empirical signature entropy, and inflates declared equivalence by 12.5\%; operator-only retains more entropy but still leaves 238 pairwise collisions.  Placement-only and state-only surfaces show the opposite failure mode: a single field creates very large kernels.  The strengthened surface still has projected collisions at the signature-class level, but no pairwise collision over the engine-probe comparison set.

The evidence side is cross-checked without retraining on headline records.  Every counted collision has side-balanced engine support and at least two public source records; keyword/operator/yes-no witnesses are all cross-source and touch 11, 8, and 3 distinct sources.  The freeze manifest binds schema, sources, projections, feature-space crosswalk, implementation, and replay entrypoints before witness counting.

The Boundary panel is a ledger rather than a victory table.  Source removal is information deletion, not a new engine-population sample: keyword can retain as little as 2\% of original witnesses and induce new witnesses.  The yes/no projection is source-sensitive and stays out of the broad robustness claim.  The architecture basis resolves feature-family and engine-family stress, while point-learned engine-pair bases leave 48 keyword, 120 operator-only, and six yes/no held-out collisions; the certificate is therefore not a memorized repair for the four hotspot pairs.

\textbf{Finite-audit replay cost.}  The Replay panel in Table~\ref{tab:robustness} reports post-extraction deterministic replay bounds, not stochastic performance comparisons or source-recoding cost: the full paper-facing replay loads frozen maps/probes, audits projections, checks repairs, and validates deterministic SQL catalogs over 455,828 rows in 2.96~s.  The 8$\times$ projection lift repeats the relation over 2,833,152 lifted rows, or 708,288 pairwise checks per projection, in 2.35~s with prefix-scaling $R^2\geq0.98$; streaming maintenance has zero drift.

\textbf{Field certificates test an architecture hypothesis.} A certificate must match the tested projection, differ under reference contracts, separate under the proposed fields, and survive smaller-field counter-witnesses.  Placement separates keyword collisions, layer separates operator-only collisions, and layer+placement separates the 492 primary records plus the six yes/no boundary records without action-variant labels.  We report layer+placement as a uniform architecture-readable basis for this corpus, not a global vocabulary.  The hypothesis is falsifiable: operator leaves six keyword collisions, placement alone fails operator, and point-learned engine-pair bases fail held-out pairs. \section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
OptSem-C audits public contracts, not private behavior or prevalence.  Manual extraction is the main validity threat: rows, hashes, line spans, and replay checks make the corpus auditable but do not provide inter-rater agreement.  Transfer is boundary-relative; local comparisons were safe here, while cloud, connector, distributed, or adaptive comparisons should retain placement/layer plus time or observability when claims need them.  SQL execution validates probe shape, and source-density tests expose documentation coverage as a confound.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
OptSem-C turns coarse optimizer-comparison labels into an auditable, replayable denominator over declared public contracts, so comparison papers can retain boundary fields before interpreting portability, plan-evidence, or performance claims.
